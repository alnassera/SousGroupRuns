When a power outage occurs, the safety of the food in your refrigerator and freezer becomes a concern. The key factors to consider are the duration of the outage, the temperature inside the refrigerator and freezer, and the type of food stored. Generally, perishable foods are at risk if they have been exposed to temperatures above 40°F (4°C) for more than two hours.

### Assessing the Situation

1. **Duration of the Outage**: Determine how long the power was out. If it was less than four hours, most food should be safe. For outages longer than four hours, more careful evaluation is needed.

2. **Temperature Check**: If you have a thermometer in your refrigerator or freezer, check the temperature. The refrigerator should be at or below 40°F (4°C), and the freezer should be at or below 0°F (-18°C). If the temperature has risen above these levels, some foods may no longer be safe.

3. **Condition of the Food**: Examine the food for signs of spoilage, such as unusual odors, colors, or textures. If any food looks or smells off, it’s best to err on the side of caution and discard it.

### What to Keep

- **Hard Cheeses**: These can generally withstand higher temperatures for a short period. If they have been above 40°F (4°C) for less than two hours, they should be safe.

- **Condiments**: Items like ketchup, mustard, and vinegar-based dressings are usually safe due to their high acidity.

- **Fruits and Vegetables**: Fresh produce can often be kept unless it shows signs of spoilage.

- **Bread and Baked Goods**: These are generally safe unless they have visible mold.

### What to Toss

- **Meat, Poultry, and Seafood**: If these have been above 40°F (4°C) for more than two hours, they should be discarded.

- **Dairy Products**: Milk, cream, and soft cheeses should be thrown away if they have been above 40°F (4°C) for more than two hours.

- **Eggs**: If eggs have been above 40°F (4°C) for more than two hours, they should be discarded.

- **Leftovers**: Any cooked food or leftovers that have been above 40°F (4°C) for more than two hours should be discarded.

### Freezer Considerations

1. **Frozen Foods**: If the freezer is full, it can keep food safe for about 48 hours (24 hours if half-full) if the door remains closed. Check for ice crystals on the food; if present, the food is likely safe to refreeze.

2. **Partial Thawing**: Foods that have partially thawed but still contain ice crystals can be refrozen, although the quality may suffer.

3. **Completely Thawed Foods**: If any food has completely thawed and been above 40°F (4°C) for more than two hours, it should be discarded.

### Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Opening the Door Frequently**: Each time the refrigerator or freezer door is opened, cold air escapes, raising the internal temperature. Try to keep the doors closed as much as possible during an outage.

- **Assuming All Foods Are Safe**: Some foods may appear safe but can harbor bacteria if they have been at unsafe temperatures for too long.

- **Refreezing Without Checking**: Always check for ice crystals before refreezing thawed foods to ensure they have not been at unsafe temperatures.

### Practical Tips for Future Preparedness

- **Invest in Appliance Thermometers**: These can help you monitor the temperature inside your refrigerator and freezer during an outage.

- **Keep Ice Packs Handy**: Having ice packs or frozen water bottles can help maintain the temperature in your refrigerator and freezer during an outage.

- **Plan Ahead**: If you know a storm or outage is likely, try to consume perishable items first and stock up on non-perishable foods.

### Conclusion

Deciding what to keep or toss after a power outage involves careful consideration of the duration of the outage, the temperature of your appliances, and the type of food. By following these guidelines, you can minimize waste while ensuring food safety. Always prioritize health and safety over potential food waste, as consuming spoiled food can lead to foodborne illnesses.
