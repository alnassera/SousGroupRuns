When the power goes out, it's understandable to worry about the safety of your food. The main things to consider are how long the power was out, the temperature inside your fridge and freezer, and what types of food you have. Perishable foods can become unsafe if they're above 40°F (4°C) for more than two hours.

### Assessing the Situation

1. **Duration of the Outage**: First, figure out how long the power was out. If it was under four hours, most of your food should be fine. If it was longer, you'll need to be more cautious.

2. **Temperature Check**: If you have a thermometer, check the temperature. Your fridge should be at or below 40°F (4°C), and your freezer should be at or below 0°F (-18°C). If it's warmer, some foods might not be safe anymore.

3. **Condition of the Food**: Look at your food for any signs of spoilage, like strange smells, colors, or textures. If anything seems off, it's better to be safe and throw it out.

### What to Keep

- **Hard Cheeses**: These can handle higher temperatures for a short time. If they've been above 40°F (4°C) for less than two hours, they're probably okay.

- **Condiments**: Things like ketchup, mustard, and vinegar-based dressings are usually safe because they're acidic.

- **Fruits and Vegetables**: Fresh produce is often fine unless it looks spoiled.

- **Bread and Baked Goods**: These are generally safe unless you see mold.

### What to Toss

- **Meat, Poultry, and Seafood**: If these have been above 40°F (4°C) for more than two hours, it's best to discard them.

- **Dairy Products**: Milk, cream, and soft cheeses should be thrown out if they've been above 40°F (4°C) for more than two hours.

- **Eggs**: If eggs have been above 40°F (4°C) for more than two hours, they should be discarded.

- **Leftovers**: Any cooked food or leftovers that have been above 40°F (4°C) for more than two hours should be discarded.

### Freezer Considerations

1. **Frozen Foods**: A full freezer can keep food safe for about 48 hours (24 hours if half-full) if you keep the door closed. Look for ice crystals; if you see them, the food is likely safe to refreeze.

2. **Partial Thawing**: Foods that have partially thawed but still have ice crystals can be refrozen, though the quality might not be as good.

3. **Completely Thawed Foods**: If any food has completely thawed and been above 40°F (4°C) for more than two hours, it should be discarded.

### Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Opening the Door Frequently**: Every time you open the fridge or freezer, cold air escapes, raising the temperature. Try to keep the doors closed as much as possible.

- **Assuming All Foods Are Safe**: Some foods might look fine but can still harbor bacteria if they've been at unsafe temperatures too long.

- **Refreezing Without Checking**: Always check for ice crystals before refreezing thawed foods to ensure they haven't been at unsafe temperatures.

### Practical Tips for Future Preparedness

- **Invest in Appliance Thermometers**: These can help you monitor the temperature inside your fridge and freezer during an outage.

- **Keep Ice Packs Handy**: Having ice packs or frozen water bottles can help maintain the temperature in your fridge and freezer during an outage.

- **Plan Ahead**: If you know a storm or outage is likely, try to consume perishable items first and stock up on non-perishable foods.

### Conclusion

Deciding what to keep or toss after a power outage requires careful thought about the outage duration, appliance temperatures, and food types. By following these guidelines, you can minimize waste while ensuring food safety. Always prioritize health and safety over potential food waste, as consuming spoiled food can lead to foodborne illnesses.
