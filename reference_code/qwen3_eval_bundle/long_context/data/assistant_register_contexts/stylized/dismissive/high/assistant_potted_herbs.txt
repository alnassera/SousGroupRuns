Keeping potted kitchen herbs like basil, mint, and parsley alive indoors isn't rocket science, but it does require some basic care. Focus on light, watering, drainage, and pruning. That's pretty much it.

### Light Requirements

#### Natural Light
Most kitchen herbs need bright, indirect sunlight. Here’s the rundown:
- **Basil**: Needs 6-8 hours of bright light daily. A south-facing window works.
- **Mint**: Can handle lower light but should still get 4-6 hours of indirect sunlight.
- **Parsley**: About 4-6 hours of light is fine; east or west-facing windows are good enough.

#### Artificial Light
If natural light isn’t cutting it, use grow lights:
- **LED Grow Lights**: They’re energy-efficient and work for all herbs. Just keep them 6-12 inches above the plants.
- **Fluorescent Lights**: Cheaper but need to be closer (2-6 inches) and will need replacing more often.

### Watering Practices

#### Frequency
Watering is crucial. Here’s what to do:
- **Check Soil Moisture**: Stick your finger in the top inch of soil. If it’s dry, water. Overwatering is a common mistake that leads to root rot.
- **Watering Schedule**: Generally, every 5-7 days is fine, but adjust based on humidity and temperature. Cut back in winter.

#### Method
- **Watering Technique**: Water until it drains from the bottom. This ensures the roots get moisture.
- **Avoid Watering Leaves**: Watering leaves can cause fungal issues. Stick to the soil.

### Drainage Considerations

Proper drainage is a must to avoid waterlogging:
- **Pot Selection**: Use pots with drainage holes. This lets excess water escape.
- **Soil Type**: A well-draining potting mix is essential. Use a mix for herbs or add perlite or sand to standard soil.
- **Saucers**: If you use saucers, empty them after watering to avoid standing water.

### Pruning and Harvesting

Regular pruning helps your herbs grow better:
- **When to Prune**: Start when the plant is about 6 inches tall. Pinch off the top leaves to encourage bushiness.
- **Harvesting**: Take leaves as needed, but don’t remove more than a third at once. This keeps the plant healthy.
- **Flowering**: If your herbs flower, pinch off the flowers. They can reduce leaf production and change flavor.

### Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Overwatering**: This is the biggest mistake. Always check soil moisture first.
- **Insufficient Light**: If your herbs look leggy or pale, they need more light. Move them or add grow lights.
- **Neglecting Pruning**: Not pruning leads to weak plants. Regular trimming keeps them healthy.
- **Ignoring Pests**: Watch for pests like aphids or spider mites. Treat them quickly with insecticidal soap or neem oil.

### Environmental Factors

#### Temperature
- **Ideal Range**: Most herbs like it between 65°F and 75°F (18°C to 24°C). Keep them away from drafts and heaters.

#### Humidity
- **Humidity Levels**: Indoor air can get dry, especially in winter. Use a humidity tray or mist the plants occasionally.

### Seasonal Considerations

- **Winter Care**: Light drops in winter, and heating can dry the air. Adjust watering and move plants closer to light.
- **Summer Care**: In summer, herbs may need more water due to evaporation. Keep an eye on soil moisture.

### Fertilization

Herbs can thrive in good soil but might need occasional fertilization:
- **Type of Fertilizer**: Use a balanced, water-soluble fertilizer diluted to half strength. Organic options like fish emulsion work too.
- **Frequency**: Fertilize every 4-6 weeks during spring and summer. Cut back in fall and winter when growth slows.

### Conclusion

Keeping potted kitchen herbs alive indoors is about managing light, water, drainage, and care. Understand what each herb needs and avoid common mistakes, and you’ll have fresh herbs for cooking all year. Just pay attention to their environment and growth, and you’ll be fine.
