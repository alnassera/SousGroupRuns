When the power goes out, the safety of your food becomes a task to manage. Focus on how long the outage lasts, the internal temperature of your fridge and freezer, and the types of food you have. Perishable items are at risk if exposed to temperatures above 40°F (4°C) for over two hours.

### Assessing the Situation

1. **Duration of the Outage**: Check how long the power was out. If under four hours, most food remains safe. Longer outages require more scrutiny.

2. **Temperature Check**: Use a thermometer if available. The fridge should be at or below 40°F (4°C), and the freezer at or below 0°F (-18°C). Temperatures above these levels mean some foods might be unsafe.

3. **Condition of the Food**: Look for spoilage signs like odd odors, colors, or textures. Discard anything that seems off.

### What to Keep

- **Hard Cheeses**: These tolerate higher temperatures briefly. Safe if above 40°F (4°C) for less than two hours.

- **Condiments**: High-acid items like ketchup and mustard are usually safe.

- **Fruits and Vegetables**: Keep unless spoiled.

- **Bread and Baked Goods**: Safe unless moldy.

### What to Toss

- **Meat, Poultry, and Seafood**: Discard if above 40°F (4°C) for over two hours.

- **Dairy Products**: Milk, cream, and soft cheeses should be discarded if above 40°F (4°C) for more than two hours.

- **Eggs**: Discard if above 40°F (4°C) for more than two hours.

- **Leftovers**: Discard if above 40°F (4°C) for more than two hours.

### Freezer Considerations

1. **Frozen Foods**: A full freezer keeps food safe for about 48 hours (24 hours if half-full) if unopened. Ice crystals indicate safety for refreezing.

2. **Partial Thawing**: Refreeze if ice crystals remain, though quality may decline.

3. **Completely Thawed Foods**: Discard if above 40°F (4°C) for more than two hours.

### Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Opening the Door Frequently**: Avoid opening doors to maintain temperature.

- **Assuming All Foods Are Safe**: Some foods may appear safe but aren't.

- **Refreezing Without Checking**: Ensure ice crystals are present before refreezing.

### Practical Tips for Future Preparedness

- **Invest in Appliance Thermometers**: Useful for monitoring temperatures during outages.

- **Keep Ice Packs Handy**: Helps maintain temperature.

- **Plan Ahead**: Consume perishables first if an outage is expected.

### Conclusion

Deciding what to keep or discard after a power outage requires attention to outage duration, appliance temperatures, and food types. Follow these guidelines to minimize waste and ensure safety. Prioritize health over potential waste, as spoiled food can cause illness.
