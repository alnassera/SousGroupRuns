When the power goes out, it's natural to feel a bit worried about the safety of the food in your refrigerator and freezer. Let's gently walk through what you can do to ensure everything stays safe and sound. The main things to keep in mind are how long the power has been out, the temperature inside your appliances, and the types of food you have stored. Perishable foods can become risky if they've been above 40°F (4°C) for more than two hours.

### Assessing the Situation

1. **Duration of the Outage**: First, take a moment to determine how long the power was out. If it was less than four hours, most of your food should be just fine. For outages longer than four hours, you'll need to take a closer look.

2. **Temperature Check**: If you have a thermometer in your refrigerator or freezer, check the temperature. Ideally, the refrigerator should be at or below 40°F (4°C), and the freezer should be at or below 0°F (-18°C). If the temperature has risen above these levels, some foods might not be safe anymore.

3. **Condition of the Food**: Take a careful look at your food. If anything has an unusual odor, color, or texture, it's best to be cautious and discard it.

### What to Keep

- **Hard Cheeses**: These are quite resilient and can handle higher temperatures for a short time. If they've been above 40°F (4°C) for less than two hours, they should be safe.

- **Condiments**: Items like ketchup, mustard, and vinegar-based dressings are usually safe because of their high acidity.

- **Fruits and Vegetables**: Fresh produce can often be kept unless it shows signs of spoilage.

- **Bread and Baked Goods**: These are generally safe unless they have visible mold.

### What to Toss

- **Meat, Poultry, and Seafood**: If these have been above 40°F (4°C) for more than two hours, it's safest to discard them.

- **Dairy Products**: Milk, cream, and soft cheeses should be thrown away if they've been above 40°F (4°C) for more than two hours.

- **Eggs**: If eggs have been above 40°F (4°C) for more than two hours, they should be discarded.

- **Leftovers**: Any cooked food or leftovers that have been above 40°F (4°C) for more than two hours should be discarded.

### Freezer Considerations

1. **Frozen Foods**: If your freezer is full, it can keep food safe for about 48 hours (24 hours if half-full) as long as the door stays closed. Look for ice crystals on the food; if they're there, the food is likely safe to refreeze.

2. **Partial Thawing**: Foods that have partially thawed but still contain ice crystals can be refrozen, although the quality might not be as good.

3. **Completely Thawed Foods**: If any food has completely thawed and been above 40°F (4°C) for more than two hours, it should be discarded.

### Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Opening the Door Frequently**: Each time you open the refrigerator or freezer door, cold air escapes, raising the internal temperature. Try to keep the doors closed as much as possible during an outage.

- **Assuming All Foods Are Safe**: Some foods may appear safe but can harbor bacteria if they've been at unsafe temperatures for too long.

- **Refreezing Without Checking**: Always check for ice crystals before refreezing thawed foods to ensure they haven't been at unsafe temperatures.

### Practical Tips for Future Preparedness

- **Invest in Appliance Thermometers**: These can help you monitor the temperature inside your refrigerator and freezer during an outage.

- **Keep Ice Packs Handy**: Having ice packs or frozen water bottles can help maintain the temperature in your refrigerator and freezer during an outage.

- **Plan Ahead**: If you know a storm or outage is likely, try to consume perishable items first and stock up on non-perishable foods.

### Conclusion

Deciding what to keep or toss after a power outage involves careful consideration of the duration of the outage, the temperature of your appliances, and the type of food. By following these guidelines, you can minimize waste while ensuring food safety. Always prioritize health and safety over potential food waste, as consuming spoiled food can lead to foodborne illnesses.
