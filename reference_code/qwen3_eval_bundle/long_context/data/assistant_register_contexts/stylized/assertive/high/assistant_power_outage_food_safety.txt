When a power outage hits, you must prioritize the safety of your food. Focus on the outage duration, internal temperatures, and food types. Perishable items are at risk if exposed to temperatures above 40°F (4°C) for over two hours.

### Assessing the Situation

1. **Duration of the Outage**: Pin down the outage length. If it's under four hours, most food remains safe. Beyond four hours, scrutinize more closely.

2. **Temperature Check**: Use a thermometer. Your refrigerator should stay at or below 40°F (4°C), and the freezer at or below 0°F (-18°C). If temperatures exceed these, some foods are compromised.

3. **Condition of the Food**: Inspect for spoilage signs—unusual odors, colors, or textures. If anything seems off, discard it without hesitation.

### What to Keep

- **Hard Cheeses**: These endure higher temperatures briefly. Safe if above 40°F (4°C) for less than two hours.

- **Condiments**: High-acid items like ketchup and mustard are generally safe.

- **Fruits and Vegetables**: Keep unless visibly spoiled.

- **Bread and Baked Goods**: Safe unless moldy.

### What to Toss

- **Meat, Poultry, and Seafood**: Discard if above 40°F (4°C) for over two hours.

- **Dairy Products**: Milk, cream, and soft cheeses must go if above 40°F (4°C) for more than two hours.

- **Eggs**: Discard if above 40°F (4°C) for over two hours.

- **Leftovers**: Toss any cooked food above 40°F (4°C) for more than two hours.

### Freezer Considerations

1. **Frozen Foods**: A full freezer keeps food safe for 48 hours (24 if half-full) if unopened. Ice crystals indicate safety for refreezing.

2. **Partial Thawing**: Refreeze if ice crystals remain, though quality may drop.

3. **Completely Thawed Foods**: Discard if above 40°F (4°C) for over two hours.

### Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Opening the Door Frequently**: Keep doors shut to maintain cold temperatures.

- **Assuming All Foods Are Safe**: Some may harbor bacteria despite appearances.

- **Refreezing Without Checking**: Confirm ice crystals before refreezing.

### Practical Tips for Future Preparedness

- **Invest in Appliance Thermometers**: Essential for monitoring temperatures during outages.

- **Keep Ice Packs Handy**: Use ice packs or frozen bottles to maintain cold.

- **Plan Ahead**: Consume perishables first and stock non-perishables if an outage looms.

### Conclusion

Decide what to keep or toss by evaluating outage duration, appliance temperatures, and food types. Follow these guidelines to minimize waste and ensure safety. Prioritize health over potential waste, as spoiled food can cause illness.
