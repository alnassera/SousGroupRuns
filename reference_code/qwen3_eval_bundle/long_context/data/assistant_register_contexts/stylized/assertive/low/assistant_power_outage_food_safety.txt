When there's a power outage, you might find yourself a bit unsure about the safety of the food in your refrigerator and freezer. It's probably important to think about how long the power was out, the temperature inside your appliances, and the types of food you have. Generally speaking, perishable foods could be at risk if they've been above 40°F (4°C) for more than two hours, but it's not always easy to be certain.

### Assessing the Situation

1. **Duration of the Outage**: You might want to figure out how long the power was out. If it was less than four hours, most food should be okay, but if it was longer, you might need to be more cautious.

2. **Temperature Check**: If you happen to have a thermometer in your refrigerator or freezer, it could be helpful to check the temperature. Ideally, the refrigerator should be at or below 40°F (4°C), and the freezer at or below 0°F (-18°C). If the temperature has gone above these levels, some foods might not be safe anymore.

3. **Condition of the Food**: It might be wise to look at the food for any signs of spoilage, like unusual odors, colors, or textures. If anything seems off, it might be better to discard it, just to be safe.

### What to Keep

- **Hard Cheeses**: These might be okay if they've been above 40°F (4°C) for less than two hours.

- **Condiments**: Things like ketchup, mustard, and vinegar-based dressings are usually safe because of their acidity.

- **Fruits and Vegetables**: Fresh produce might be kept unless it shows spoilage.

- **Bread and Baked Goods**: These are generally safe unless there's visible mold.

### What to Toss

- **Meat, Poultry, and Seafood**: If these have been above 40°F (4°C) for more than two hours, it might be best to discard them.

- **Dairy Products**: Milk, cream, and soft cheeses should probably be thrown away if they've been above 40°F (4°C) for more than two hours.

- **Eggs**: If eggs have been above 40°F (4°C) for more than two hours, they should likely be discarded.

- **Leftovers**: Any cooked food or leftovers that have been above 40°F (4°C) for more than two hours should probably be discarded.

### Freezer Considerations

1. **Frozen Foods**: If the freezer is full, it might keep food safe for about 48 hours (24 hours if half-full) if the door stays closed. Checking for ice crystals on the food could indicate it's safe to refreeze.

2. **Partial Thawing**: Foods that have partially thawed but still have ice crystals might be refrozen, though the quality could suffer.

3. **Completely Thawed Foods**: If any food has completely thawed and been above 40°F (4°C) for more than two hours, it should probably be discarded.

### Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Opening the Door Frequently**: Each time the refrigerator or freezer door is opened, cold air escapes, which might raise the internal temperature. It might be best to keep the doors closed as much as possible during an outage.

- **Assuming All Foods Are Safe**: Some foods might appear safe but could harbor bacteria if they've been at unsafe temperatures for too long.

- **Refreezing Without Checking**: Always checking for ice crystals before refreezing thawed foods might help ensure they haven't been at unsafe temperatures.

### Practical Tips for Future Preparedness

- **Invest in Appliance Thermometers**: These could help you monitor the temperature inside your refrigerator and freezer during an outage.

- **Keep Ice Packs Handy**: Having ice packs or frozen water bottles might help maintain the temperature in your refrigerator and freezer during an outage.

- **Plan Ahead**: If you know a storm or outage is likely, it might be a good idea to consume perishable items first and stock up on non-perishable foods.

### Conclusion

Deciding what to keep or toss after a power outage might involve careful consideration of the duration of the outage, the temperature of your appliances, and the type of food. By following these guidelines, you might minimize waste while ensuring food safety. Always prioritizing health and safety over potential food waste could be wise, as consuming spoiled food might lead to foodborne illnesses.
