So, when the power goes out, you might start worrying about the food in your fridge and freezer. The big things to think about are how long the power's been out, what the temperature is inside your appliances, and what kind of food you've got in there. If perishable foods have been sitting above 40°F (4°C) for more than two hours, they could be in trouble.

### Checking Things Out

1. **How Long Was the Power Out?**: First, figure out how long the power was off. If it was under four hours, most of your food should be okay. But if it was longer, you'll need to take a closer look.

2. **Temperature Check**: If you've got a thermometer in your fridge or freezer, use it to check the temperature. Your fridge should be at or below 40°F (4°C), and the freezer should be at or below 0°F (-18°C). If it's warmer than that, some of your food might not be safe anymore.

3. **Look at the Food**: Check your food for any weird smells, colors, or textures. If something seems off, it's better to be safe and toss it.

### What You Can Keep

- **Hard Cheeses**: These can handle a bit of warmth for a short time. If they've been above 40°F (4°C) for less than two hours, they're probably fine.

- **Condiments**: Things like ketchup, mustard, and vinegar-based dressings usually stay safe because they're pretty acidic.

- **Fruits and Veggies**: Fresh produce is often okay unless it looks spoiled.

- **Bread and Baked Goods**: These are generally safe unless you see mold.

### What You Should Toss

- **Meat, Poultry, and Seafood**: If these have been above 40°F (4°C) for more than two hours, it's time to let them go.

- **Dairy Products**: Milk, cream, and soft cheeses should be thrown out if they've been above 40°F (4°C) for more than two hours.

- **Eggs**: If eggs have been above 40°F (4°C) for more than two hours, they need to be discarded.

- **Leftovers**: Any cooked food or leftovers that have been above 40°F (4°C) for more than two hours should be tossed.

### Freezer Tips

1. **Frozen Foods**: A full freezer can keep food safe for about 48 hours (24 hours if it's half-full) if you keep the door shut. Look for ice crystals on the food; if you see them, the food is probably safe to refreeze.

2. **Partially Thawed Foods**: If food has partially thawed but still has ice crystals, you can refreeze it, though the quality might not be as good.

3. **Completely Thawed Foods**: If any food has completely thawed and been above 40°F (4°C) for more than two hours, it should be thrown out.

### Mistakes to Avoid

- **Opening the Door Too Much**: Every time you open the fridge or freezer door, cold air escapes, and the temperature goes up. Try to keep the doors closed as much as you can during an outage.

- **Thinking All Foods Are Safe**: Some foods might look okay but can still have bacteria if they've been at unsafe temperatures too long.

- **Refreezing Without Checking**: Always check for ice crystals before refreezing thawed foods to make sure they haven't been at unsafe temperatures.

### Getting Ready for Next Time

- **Get Appliance Thermometers**: These can help you keep an eye on the temperature inside your fridge and freezer during an outage.

- **Have Ice Packs Ready**: Ice packs or frozen water bottles can help keep your fridge and freezer cool during an outage.

- **Plan Ahead**: If you know a storm or outage is coming, try to eat perishable items first and stock up on non-perishable foods.

### Wrapping It Up

Deciding what to keep or toss after a power outage means thinking about how long the power was out, the temperature of your appliances, and the type of food. By following these tips, you can cut down on waste while keeping your food safe. Always put health and safety first, because eating spoiled food can make you sick.
